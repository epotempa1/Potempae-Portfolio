KitchenMate

Guiding users to a healthier, more sustainable lifestyle.

The Challenge

For Interaction Design I worked in a group of five other people to create a cooking app for the Amazon Echo Show called, "Kitchen Mate." Kitchen Mate aimed to guide users to a healthier, more sustainable way of life. We conducted several observations and interviews to gain a comprehensive understanding about diet, cooking, and lifestyle habits. The data was used as a guidance for the duration of the project to maintain a user-centered approach to design.

Client

Interaction Design Project at City, University of London

Timeline

September, 2022-December, 2022.

Phase One : The Research

Interviews and Observations

KitchenMate's design was guided by data gathered from semi-structured interviews and contextual inquiries. Following a collaborative brainstorming session, we created a list of open-ended questions to serve as a guide for the interviewing process. Topics of interest centered on: cooking habits, grocery shopping routines, dietary requirements, and sustainability. Interviews were one on one, occured both remotely and in person, and were under an hour long.

Contextual Inquiries allowed us to have a dialogue with our participants as we observed them preparing and cooking meals. During these sessions, we documented any pertenent comments, behaviors, and frustrations, and would ask probing or clarifiying questions when appropriate. Inquiries occurred in various settings including: a student hall kitchen and a single family home.

Phase Two : Analyzing the Data

Each member of the group began the analysis by familiarizing themselves with the data individually, paying attention to key insights. Afterwards, we reconvened to discuss our findings and organized our thoughts into an affinity diagram to identify overlaps amongst our findings. Some common themes we identified included: the desire to quick and easy meals, simple nutrition tracking, and preferring online recipes over hardcopy books.

We then moved on to developing an empathy map, to organize what our users think, do, feel,and say. This gave us a clear starting point and served as a foundation to our personas.

Phase Three : Who are the Users?

The data gathered from our semis-tructured interviews and observations were used to bring our two personas, Maria and Brian, to life. From our data, we realized there were two different types of users: one who is already knowledgeable about the ins and outs of a healthy lifestyle, and one who is at the beginning stages of their journey.

Brian

Brian is a former athlete already well versed in fitness and nutrition. His hectic schedule as an arbitrator in London, however, has limited his capactiy to maintain that lifestyle and he's frustrated that he feels he must choose between a healthy meal and something delicious. Ideally he doesn't want to spend more than twenty minutes cooking a meal, and wants a variety of healthy and delicious options that meet his nutritional goals.

×

Maria

Maria was interested in shifting to a more environmentally friendly, vegan lifestyle and was looking for optimal alternatives to animal products. She feels overwhelmed though and doesn't know where to start when looking for replacement products, and misses meals she used to enjoy but had to give up because they are not vegan. She doesn't mind spending more time cooking her meals and likes the idea of tracking her vitamins and minerals.

Phase Four : Ideation

Brainstorming and Sketching

We started this stage by independently brainstorming ideas to solve user pain points and presented them to the group via sketches, each group member having three to four ideas. From there, we moved onto storyboarding where we more clearly outlined how KitchenMate could address these painpoints. Because our prior research indicated that people tend to struggle reading nutritional information on the packaging, the first journey we illustrated depicted a consumer in a grocery store scanning a food item to gain further information (calorie information, vegetarian vs. vegan product, etc.). The second focused on how users could take advantage of KitchenMate's extensive recipe library as they browsed to find one that most suited their needs.

Phase Five : Wireframes and a Prototype

We initiated this process by creating hand drawn wireframes, affording us the flexibility to make adjustments as needed. Once completed, we moved on to prototyping and created prototypes for the following user journeys:

      Onboarding
      Cooking a Recipe
      Custom Recipes
      Adding a Reipe to the Daily Log

We ensured our prototype would be intuitive and learnable by incorporating pre-existing and commonly accepted design principles such as: drop down menus, text fields, and navigation bars.

The prototype was divided into four sections: The Homepage, Dashboard, My Recipes, and Grocery List.

The Homepage

At the homepage, users can review recommended recipes generated by preferences they chose during the onboarding process. They can also see their daily log and get an idea of nutritional goals for the day

In the top right, the "my account" button allows users to change meal preferences, change their health information, or add other users for easy access between accounts.

In the top right, the "my account" button allows users to change meal preferences, change their health information, or add other users for easy access between accounts.

Recipe Page

A common theme shared amongst our interviewees was the desire to search for recipes online. This lead to the development of a core element for KitchenMate: a library of recipes. When viewing their options, users will see familiar features such as a list of ingredients, cook time, and nutritional facts about the meal (vegan, high protein, nut free, etc.). They can broaden or narrow their search by adjusting these filters or the slider signifying cook time. This can be accomplished manually either through Echo Show’s touch screen, or by voice command. This was a crucial attribute to include in our final product as our user research indicated that no two individuals’ cooking habits are alike. We wanted to ensure our product would appeal to both those who enjoy spending hours in the kitchen, and people who prefer to allocate their time elsewhere. .

Step-by-step Instructions

Two concerns revealed in our research were: concerns the desire to clearly view each cooking step, and eliminate excessive amounts of information. Kitchenmate addresses this by using a step-by-step guide conveying the process on a clear modal screen with a progress bar at the top indicating which step they are on.

Adding to the daily log

For those who are counting calories or tracking their diets, KitchenMate automatically provides them with the option to add a meal to their daily log for easy tracking.

Enviornmental Tips

While our research indicated that users were not particularly concerned about the environmental impact of their eating habits, sustainability was still a goal for the ficticious client behind KitchenMate. As a result, we still implemented environmentally friendly features, but less obtrusively. When a user has finished cooking a meal, they are presented with a modal with a short tip about living more sustainably. Users then have the option to click for more information if they choose to.

Ingredients List

A unique aspect of KitchenMate is the ability to add desired ingredients to a shopping list with a simple click or voice command. From there, users can pull up the app on their mobiles when they arrive at the grocery store for an easy checklist. They also can scan barcodes to get more information about certain products, which is appealing to those who expressed the desire to shop for cage free eggs, for example, or are perhaps attempting to follow a vegan diet but are struggling to find quality protein replacements for meat.

Phase Seven : Testing & Reflection

Testing with Users

At the beginning of the test, we explained the purpose of KitchenMate and the evaluation, discussed consent, and asked if they had any questions. Once testing had completed, we asked users open-ended questions about their overall experience with KitchenMate. We wanted to know what they enjoyed, disliked, and what they would like to see change.

User testing showed the app was clear and easy to navigate as each participant accomplished the tasks in less than five minutes. Additionally, they praised KitchenMate's intuitiveness, emphasizing the familiarity of icons and available filters. We were moderately concerned that the placement of the "Add to Grocery List" button may stir confusion. In our original markups it was placed next to the recipe ingredients, but for testing we placed it next to the "Cook Now" button instead. Thankfully, participants experience no frustration or confusion due to this placement.

Areas for Improvement

While we were overall satisfied with our design and felt it successfully addressed all pain points discovered in our research, we felt it could have been improved had we added more cues or suggesting recipes for those indecisive users. Additionally, we could have taken advantage of the Echo Show's larger screen size by adding more dimensionality to our designs and layouts, instead of relying on a traditional grid pattern. Additionally, the lack of an explicit "final" screen to indicate the user had completed certain tasks, (cooking a meal, for example) could lead to some confusion.

Due to time and resource constraints, we had three observers for one contextual enquiry of two people which could have led our recruits to stray from their natural behavior. While having multiple researchers present may have alleviated concerns of observer bias, having a maximum of two researchers present would have been ideal.
